CPR Score

Term Name Definition How It’s Measured
Compression Depth The depth of chest compressions delivered during CPR. Measured in inches. Adult CPR guidelines recommend 2.0–2.4 inches (5–6 cm) for proper depth (AHA, ERC, ARC 2020).
Compression Rate The number of compressions performed per minute. Measured in compressions per minute; optimal range is 100-120/min according to AHA, ERC, and ARC 2020 guidelines.
Recoil The extent to which the chest fully recoils after each compression. Measured as a percentage of full recoil; 100% recoil is ideal.
Hand Position The placement of hands on the chest during compressions. Assessed by checking alignment with the sternum’s center.
Hand-off Time The time spent without performing chest compressions. Measured in seconds; minimizing this time improves CPR effectiveness.
CCF (Chest Compression Fraction) The percentage of CPR time spent delivering chest compressions. Expressed as a percentage of total CPR duration. Higher values correlate with better outcomes.
Compression Count The total number of compressions performed during a CPR session. Measured as a count of individual compressions performed. For example, in a 30:2 guideline, performing 31+ compressions results in 'too many', while fewer than 30 is 'too few'.
Ventilation Count The total number of rescue breaths given during a CPR session. Measured as a count of individual breaths delivered. For a 30:2 guideline, providing fewer than 2 ventilations is 'too few', and more than 2 is 'too many'.
Ventilation Rate The number of ventilations delivered per minute. Measured in breaths per minute (bpm). AHA and ERC recommend 10-12 bpm for adults.
Ventilation Volume The amount of air delivered per ventilation. Measured in volume per breath. Guidelines recommend approximately 400–600 mL per breath (AHA, ARC 2020).
Ventilation Pressure The speed at which air is delivered during ventilation. Measured in liters per second (L/s). Ensuring proper delivery speed prevents lung overexpansion and barotrauma.

CPR Metrics

Metric Name Definition Why It Matters How It’s Measured
Compression Depth Displays the percentage of total compressions that were too shallow, good, or too deep. Ensures effective blood circulation; compressions that are too shallow reduce perfusion, and too deep can cause injury. Displayed as a percentage breakdown of compressions in each category (e.g., Too shallow: X%, Good: Y%, Too deep: Z%).
Compression Release Displays the percentage of total compressions that had incomplete release versus good release. Incomplete release prevents full cardiac refill, reducing the effectiveness of CPR. Displayed as a percentage breakdown of compressions with incomplete release versus good release.
Compression Rate Displays the percentage of total compressions that were too slow, good, or too fast. Maintaining a correct compression rate optimizes perfusion and improves survival chances. Displayed as a percentage breakdown of compressions that were too slow, good, or too fast.
Hand Position Displays the percentage of total compressions that were applied to the abdomen (incorrect), in the correct position (good), or too far left/right (incorrect). Correct hand position ensures effective compression and reduces the risk of injury. Displayed as a percentage breakdown of compressions by incorrect (abdomen), correct (good), or incorrect (left/right) hand placement.
Compression Count Displays the percentage of total compressions that were too few, good, or too many according to CPR guidelines (e.g., 30:2 ratio). Maintaining the correct number of compressions per cycle ensures adherence to CPR guidelines. Displayed as a percentage breakdown of total compressions by too few, good, or too many.
Ventilation Volume Displays the percentage of total ventilations that had too little volume, good volume, or too much volume. Proper ventilation volume prevents lung injury and ensures effective oxygenation. Displayed as a percentage breakdown of ventilations by too little, good, or too much volume.
Ventilation Count Displays the percentage of total ventilations that were too few, good, or too many according to CPR guidelines (e.g., 30:2 ratio). Correct ventilation count ensures proper oxygen delivery without over-ventilation or under-ventilation. Displayed as a percentage breakdown of total ventilations by too few, good, or too many.
